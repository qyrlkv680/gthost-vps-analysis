## The Not-So-Good: Complaints Worth Taking Seriously

Pretending the bad reviews don't exist would defeat the purpose of reading Trustpilot in the first place. A few patterns stand out, and each one tells you something specific about the risk profile of signing up.

**Stripe payment rejections.** The most pointed one-star review complains that GTHost uses Stripe for card processing, that Stripe "denies almost everyone's cards," and that the result is a locked account and an offline business "for hours." This is a real friction point if your card issuer is skittish about international or recurring charges — though it's worth noting GTHost also supports other payment methods, and a few reviewers mentioned wishing they could pay in crypto.

**Policy and configuration drift.** A long-term customer who started with five servers and wound down to one wrote in February 2026 that "they always change their policies for the worst" and that recurring server-side problems forced them to absorb replacement costs. Another reviewer from March 2026 alleged servers were being shut off the day before renewal was due. Whether these are isolated billing edge cases or a systemic pattern is hard to say from public reviews alone, but the consistency of the complaint theme is a yellow flag worth knowing about before you commit to hosting anything mission-critical without a backup plan.

**Unmanaged by default.** Several reviewers — even satisfied ones — pointed out that the dedicated servers and VPS plans are unmanaged, meaning you either need Linux server administration skills or you need to hire someone who has them. One December 2025 reviewer said the first month turned out more expensive than expected because they had to bring in an admin after delivery. This isn't a flaw in the product; it's a feature of the price point. But if you're not technical, it's the single most important thing to understand before clicking "order."

**A language mismatch in support.** One frustrated reviewer mentioned a support conversation switching to Ukrainian without being asked. This appears to be an isolated incident rather than a pattern, but it's the kind of detail that reminds you GTHost operates a globally distributed support operation, and the experience can vary by who picks up the ticket.
